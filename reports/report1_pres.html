Birth decline in Switzerland

1 Objectives

  • Quantify Switzerland’s birth deficit over 2000–2024, adjusting for demographic changes and delayed childbearing
  • Examine temporal and geographical heterogeneity in birth deficits
  • Explore socio-economic and policy factors associated with variations in excess/deficit births

2 Data

2.1 Types of data

  • Birth data:
    • Individual-level: year, month, municipality, maternal age
    • Period: 1987–2024 (analysis uses 2000–2024)
  • Population data:
    • Aggregated: year × canton, nationality (Swiss vs non-Swiss), 1987–2024
    • Optionally: municipality-level counts 2011–2024
  • Socio-economic data (Explanatory variables)
    • Votation data (municipality-level):
      • family/education support
      • paternity leave
      • childcare tax reductions
      • women’s suffrage
    • Swiss Household data (canton-level)
    • Socio-economic position (municipality-level)

3 Methods

3.1 Birth prediction model

We model age-specific births using a negative binomial regression with an overdispersion parameter. Let:

  • \(y_{a,t}\) = number of births at maternal age \(a\) in year \(t\)
  • \(N_{a,t}\) = population of women at age \(a\) in year \(t\)

Then:

\[ y_{a,t} \sim \text{NegBinom}(\mu_{a,t}, \phi), \quad \log\mu_{a,t} = f(a + \delta_t) + \gamma_m + \log N_{a,t}, \]

where:

  • \(f(a)\) is the age-specific fertility function modeled with a Gaussian Process (GP),
  • \(\delta_t\) is the calendar-year-specific age shift capturing delayed childbearing (also modeled with a GP),
  • \(\gamma_m\) is a fixed effect for calendar month \(m\),
  • \(\phi\) is the negative binomial overdispersion parameter.

This formulation allows the fertility curve to shift over time while accounting for seasonality and overdispersion in birth counts.

3.2 Excess births

3.2.1 National-level

Let \(\hat{\mu}_{a,t}\) = posterior predicted births from the model, the excess is:

\[ \text{Excess}_{a,t} = y_{a,t} - \hat{\mu}_{a,t}, \quad \text{Relative Excess}_{a,t} = \frac{y_{a,t} - \hat{\mu}_{a,t}}{\hat{\mu}_{a,t}}. \]

This gives the absolute and relative deviations between observed and expected births.

3.2.2 District-level

Let \(d\) denote a district and \(y_{d,a,t}\) the observed births in district \(d\) (unknown at prediction stage). We distribute the national posterior predicted births \(\hat{\mu}_{a,t}\) across districts using a multinomial distribution:

\[ \hat{\mu}_{1\cdots D,a,t} \sim \text{Multinomial}\Big(\hat{\mu}_{a,t},p_{1\cdots D,a,t} \Big). \]

Where \(p_{d,a,t}\) is the proportion of women of age \(a\) in district \(d\) relative to the total national population of age \(a\):

\[ p_{d,a,t} = \frac{N_{d,a,t}}{\sum_{d'} N_{d',a,t}}. \]

The district-level excess is then:

\[ \text{Excess}_{d,a,t} = y_{d,a,t} - \hat{\mu}_{d,a,t}, \quad \text{Relative Excess}_{d,a,t} = \frac{y_{d,a,t} - \hat{\mu}_{d,a,t}}{\hat{\mu}_{d,a,t}}. \]

This allows us to quantify deviations at a finer spatial scale while respecting the national posterior predictions.
